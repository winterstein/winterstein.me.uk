Anything Warm and Female






"Let's go somewhere indoors." He said, "I?m getting
cold."
"I've gotta finish this drink." I replied, "For the past
half an hour I've had a strong urge just to throw the
glass into the river and it's only the thought of the
wine in it that's stopped me."
That touched his sense of right and wrong, so we paused
for a minute whilst I emptied the glass in front of me.
It was a shot-an-a-mixer glass from the bar, half full
of cheap red wine. Jacob's Creek, 1995; a good year, but
a fucking shit vineyard. I gulped it down anyway, then
hefted the glass out over the water. It was swallowed
instantly by the darkness. I waited, constantly
expecting a splash as it landed. The splash didn't come.
And didn't come, for microseconds that were definitely
too long. Finally there was a crash and tinkle as the
glass impacted on one of the punts moored on the other
side of the river. We pictured glass shards spitting
across the unused punt, laughed and left.
        It was long past midnight then. I don't know
what time it was beyond late, but I'd spent midnight
fencing with juggling clubs. Cut and thrust, parry and
lunge. Spirit flowing, back and forth along the path and
on the grass, shouting swashbuckling things and aiming
my blows. Until my friend hacked a heavy handed parry in
too hard and too accurate, and snapped the club. Olly
hadn't joined in, but slipped off quietly to brood. Good
friend that I was, I hadn't noticed. I would have missed
him completely if it hadn't been for the broken club. It
wasn't mine, or the friend I'd been 'fighting' with, but
another friend who hadn't approved from the beginning of
his juggling clubs being put to such uses. So (my
attempts at surreptitious super-glue repairs having
failed) we had to apologise, look sufficiently guilty
for the requisite amount of time, and promise to replace
the broken club. The guilty look kept me in the room
long enough to connect with Olly again.
He walked in self-consciously down, like the world was
on his shoulders, and had invited some other heavy
celestial orbs round for the night. Olly is a little
younger than me, but with about the same level of
tarnish. He projects better, or at least further; in
this case depression. When he said he was going to sit
by the river, I thought it wise to join him. Just in
case he felt like doing something more active and
jumping in it. If he'd thought it through properly
perhaps he would of. The river was infamously shallow
near us. Wide and picturesque but shallow. A suicide
attempt would only result in muddy jeans and a ruined
T-shirt; a gloriously ironic finale. However, either the
mood left him or he just wanted the extra air down
there. Perspective is important, and a cold clear
atmosphere can do wonders. It didn't though, not this
time.
There is a heaviness which comes sometimes, a feeling of
weightlessness but far too much mass. He smiled
ruefully. A lost misfit. If only such misfits could
reject society's targets as well as society's methods.
But no, we all end up jumping through the same hoops,
just to show 'them' that we could have done it. So,
leave the straight and narrow, dare to shout, and laugh
and live, but make sure you still get a girlfriend, a
good salary, a decent car. Success is always measured in
standard issue terms. Personal terms are cut to fit.
"I just want someone." he says, legs dangling over the
river wall, and again later in my room, "Someone to want
me."
"Don't worry about it, it'll happen." I repeat, "And
anyway, having a girl isn't the be-all and end-all of
things, it just opens up a whole new set of insecurities
to get fucked up about."
He looks glum, I spout other trite but unhelpful truths.
The blood red wine is tasting better, but Olly refuses
to drink. He did have a five pint head-start to start
with and it's clearly weighing him down. His face moves
reluctantly like his belly is rolling ponderously after
it. He knows this black mood is unhelpful, knows there's
nothing to be done tonight. He can't be temporarily
happy though; he's done that too long. I try to connect,
but it's a hit and miss affair. Now and then I hit on
something, and he leans out of his chair to agree,
interrupting the downward flow of the music. Nothing I
say really helps, though trying is important: "You know
the people who love you," I tell him, "And you need two
hands to count them. You know that."
It doesn't alter his mood. There isn't really anything I
can say to help, no magic wand I can wave to solve his
problems, which go beyond a girl for the night.
"Goodnight." he says. Tomorrow he'll be smiling and
won't mention this, or will do so only to say how silly
he was. The pain remains though, but it would be too
embarrassing to own to it by day. There's nothing I can
do for him though. Nothing.
He walks out. I wait for a splash but it doesn't come.
